# Executive Summary: Order Channel Performance and Market Share Analytics
## SkyCity Auckland Restaurants & Bars

**Prepared for:** Government Stakeholders and Policy Advisors  
**Date:** May 2025  
**Classification:** Public

---

## Overview

This executive summary presents key findings from a comprehensive analysis of order channel performance across 150 SkyCity Auckland Restaurants & Bars establishments, spanning five subregions, ten cuisine types, and five business segments. The analysis examines how ordering behaviour is distributed across In-Store, Uber Eats, DoorDash, and Self-Delivery channels, and the implications for the Auckland hospitality sector.

## Key Findings

### 1. Delivery Now Dominates Auckland's Restaurant Order Volume

Delivery channels collectively account for **68.6% of all monthly orders**, with In-Store dining retaining only 31.4%. This represents a fundamental shift in how Aucklanders interact with the hospitality sector, with more than two in every three restaurant orders now involving delivery.

### 2. In-Store Dining Remains the Profitability Backbone

Despite representing less than a third of order volume, In-Store dining generates **49.6% of total net profit** across the sector. The In-Store net profit margin stands at **31.1%**, compared to just **9.1% for Uber Eats** and **8.8% for DoorDash**. Self-Delivery achieves a **22.9% margin**, positioning it as the most profitable delivery channel.

### 3. Central Auckland Leads Delivery Adoption

Central Auckland restaurants see **72.0% of orders through delivery channels**, the highest of any subregion. This compares to 67.8% for the rest of Auckland. Aggregator dependence (Uber Eats + DoorDash) in Central Auckland reaches **51.9%**, compared to 42.6% in South Auckland.

### 4. Cuisine Type Strongly Influences Channel Dependence

Pizza restaurants channel **55.2% of orders through aggregators** (Uber Eats and DoorDash), followed by Indian at 52.6%. At the other end, Seafood restaurants show the lowest aggregator reliance at 39.1%, paired with the highest Self-Delivery share (24.4%).

### 5. Channel Diversification is Broadly Healthy

The average Channel Diversification Score across all 150 restaurants is **0.980** (on a 0–1 scale), indicating well-balanced channel portfolios. No restaurants currently fall into the "High Risk" category (≥70% reliance on a single aggregator). However, the average aggregator dependence of 47.0% places the sector close to moderate risk territory.

## Strategic Implications for Policy

### Market Resilience
The broadly diversified channel structure across Auckland's restaurant sector provides a degree of natural resilience against platform-specific disruptions. However, the concentration of delivery volume through two aggregators (Uber Eats and DoorDash) creates systemic risk at the sector level.

### Commission Impact
With commission rates of 15–30% reducing aggregator channel profit margins to approximately 9%, there is a structural profitability challenge for restaurants that are increasingly dependent on these platforms. This margin pressure has implications for employment, wage levels, and business sustainability in the hospitality sector.

### Geographic Equity
The significant variation in channel preference between Central Auckland (72% delivery) and suburban areas (68% delivery) suggests that delivery infrastructure and aggregator coverage are not uniformly distributed. South Auckland's lower aggregator usage (42.6%) may reflect coverage gaps rather than preference differences.

### Self-Delivery as an Alternative
Self-Delivery's 22.9% profit margin demonstrates that viable alternatives to aggregator dependence exist. Policies that support restaurants in developing self-delivery capabilities — including access to delivery technology, workforce training, and shared logistics infrastructure — could improve sector profitability and resilience.

## Recommendations for Stakeholder Consideration

1. **Monitor aggregator market concentration** to ensure competitive commission structures and fair platform practices across Uber Eats and DoorDash.

2. **Support self-delivery infrastructure development** through industry partnerships, shared logistics platforms, or targeted business support programmes that reduce the capital barrier to self-delivery.

3. **Address geographic delivery equity** by investigating whether lower aggregator usage in South Auckland and West Auckland reflects infrastructure gaps, and consider interventions to ensure equitable access to delivery services.

4. **Commission transparency frameworks** that require aggregators to clearly communicate commission structures, algorithmic visibility factors, and data sharing practices to restaurant partners.

5. **Seasonal and trend monitoring** to track the evolution of channel shares over time, particularly the growth trajectory of Self-Delivery as a proportion of total orders.
